Day 3 — Bazaars, Bosphorus Coast & Sunset Cruise
Saturday only for the Grand Bazaar · Colleague picks woven in · Ends with the private sunset cruise
⚠ Grand Bazaar: Saturday Only

The Grand Bazaar is closed every Sunday. Today is your one and only chance — go this morning.

07:30
Breakfast at Zeyn Otel
Included. Fuel up well — this is your longest day. You can try Van Kahvaltı Evi on another morning if you want a second big breakfast experience.
Breakfast
09:30
Grand Bazaar (Kapalıçarşı)
One of the world's oldest covered markets — 15th century, 4,000 shops. Ceramics, leather, jewellery, carpets, spices. Haggling etiquette: start at ~50% of the asking price, settle warmly. Fixed-price shops (malls, modern boutiques) don't haggle.
Culture
11:30
Egyptian (Spice) Bazaar — Mısır Çarşısı
More intimate and sensory. Towers of saffron, sumac, rose petals, baklava. Sample freely. Good for gifts: spices, tea, Turkish delight. Better atmosphere than the Grand Bazaar for actually buying things.
Culture
12:30
Rüstem Paşa Mosque — Hidden Gem ⭐
Istanbul-born colleague's secret pick. Hidden above the bazaar near the Spice Bazaar — most tourists walk straight past it. Famous for extraordinary Iznik tilework covering every surface of the interior. A small mosque with an outsized visual impact. Free entry. Cover up. 15-minute detour that beats most famous sights.
Colleague Pick ⭐ · Free · Near Spice Bazaar

The real heartbeat of Istanbul lives across the Bosphorus in Kadıköy.

A mythical islet in the middle of the Bosphorus, reached by a short boat. The tower has been a lighthouse, a quarantine station, a James Bond location, and an Ottoman watchtower. The views back to Istanbul from the tower's deck are exceptional.

What to Eat
Istanbul Food Guide
From street food ratings to celebrity restaurants — curated for your honeymoon
Street Food & Local Eats — Rated by Travellers
01
Trileçe (Three-Milk Cake)
📍 Hafız Mustafa 1864 · Sirkeci
A soaked sponge cake drenched in three types of milk — intensely creamy, rich without being cloying. The standout dessert at Istanbul's most iconic sweet shop. Better than the chicken pudding if you can only order one.
🌕🌕🌕🌕🌗
ORDER THIS
02
Tavuk Göğsü (Chicken Milk Pudding)
📍 Hafız Mustafa 1864 · Sirkeci
A uniquely Turkish dessert — a silky milk pudding made with shredded chicken breast. The texture is surprisingly interesting (savoury-meets-sweet), though a full portion can feel quite heavy by the end. Worth trying a few spoonfuls.
🌕🌕🌕🌕🌗
TRY A BIT
03
Midye Dolma (Stuffed Mussels)
📍 Street vendors everywhere · Moda Midyecisi in Kadıköy
Mussels stuffed with spiced rice, pine nuts, and currants, then steamed and served with a squeeze of lemon. Rich, aromatic, and intensely satisfying. The ultimate Istanbul street snack — you'll end up with oil on your hands and a big smile. Perfect on Day 4 in Kadıköy.
🌕🌕🌕🌕🌕
MUST EAT
04
Salt Bae Steak (Nusr-Et)
📍 Nusr-Et Steakhouse
Famous for the internet spectacle — the salt-sprinkling showman. In practice, the performance far outweighs the food quality, and the prices are eye-wateringly high. Travellers consistently rate it mediocre for the cost. Save your money for the private Bosphorus cruise instead.
🌕🌕🌗🌑🌑
SKIP
05
Kokoreç (Grilled Lamb Intestines)
📍 Search "Kokoreç" on Google Maps — vendors everywhere
Istanbul's beloved street offal — seasoned lamb intestines wrapped around a spit and grilled over charcoal until the fat crisps and the spices caramelise. The local equivalent of a great sausage sandwich. Pair it with Ayran (cold salted yoghurt drink) — unusual at first sip, completely addictive after that. For adventurous eaters: do not miss this.
🌕🌕🌕🌕🌕
MUST TRY
06
Balık Dürüm (Grilled Fish Wrap)
📍 Balık Dürüm Yildirim Usta
Freshly grilled fish (usually mackerel or sea bass), tucked into a warm flatbread with salad and pickled vegetables. One of Istanbul's most beloved street meals — so good that travellers forget to photograph it before it's gone. Look for the queue and join it without hesitation.
🌕🌕🌕🌕🌕
MUST EAT
07
Clay Pot Beef + Oil-Poached Prawns
📍 Old Ottoman Cafe & Restaurant
The signature clay pot beef is slow-cooked until fall-apart tender and deeply savoury — the kind of dish that suits people who enjoy bold, umami-forward flavours. The oil-poached prawns and iron-plate aubergine are equally strong. Widely recommended by travellers who found the flavour profile approachable and familiar. Staff are warm and welcoming.
🌕🌕🌕🌕🌗
RECOMMENDED
08
Traditional Charcoal Grill (Ocakbaşı)
📍 Zübeyir Ocakbaşı · Taksim
Lamb skewers, lamb ribs, and köfte grilled over charcoal right in front of you. Straightforward, honest, and reliably delicious — this is Turkish grilled meat at its purest. Not a budget option, but a satisfying final celebratory dinner on Day 4. The lamb rib is the highlight.
🌕🌕🌕🌗🌑
SOLID CHOICE

Transport Guide

⚠ Taxi Safety — Read Before You Arrive

Never accept a "fixed rate" taxi. Never negotiate a price before getting in. If a driver refuses the meter or quotes you a flat price — get out immediately. Use BiTaksi or Uber only. Both apps record your route and fare, providing a paper trail and accountability.

🚌
Istanbulkart (Red Card)
Essential — buy on Day 1 · Works on everything
▾

The red Istanbulkart is the single most important thing to buy on arrival. Available at kiosks at any tram stop or convenience store (Migros, BIM). Load it with credit and tap for every journey — tram, metro, funicular, ferry, and bus. Far cheaper than single tickets or taxi for every short trip.

Where to buy

Tram stop kiosks (look for the IETT logo) · Convenience stores near hotel · Cost: ~£3 for card + top up with as much credit as you want

📱
BiTaksi & Uber
For all taxis — download before you land
▾

BiTaksi is the Turkish equivalent of Uber — it summons licensed yellow taxis with the meter guaranteed. Uber also operates in Istanbul. Use either app for every single taxi journey. The fare is set by the meter, your route is recorded, and the driver knows someone is watching.

NEVER do this
  • Accept a price quoted before getting in
  • Use unmarked or private cars in arrivals
  • Agree to a "shorter route" or "faster way" suggestion
🚊
Tram T1 — Sultanahmet Line
Your main line · Connects all Old City sights
▾

The T1 tram runs from Kabataş through Karaköy, across Galata Bridge, through Eminönü, and along the Old City to Sultanahmet, Çemberlitaş, and beyond. This is your daily workhorse. Sultanahmet stop is right by the Blue Mosque and Zeyn Otel. Crowded at rush hour — keep bags in front.

⚡
F2 Funicular — The Concierge Secret
Karaköy → Istiklal in 2 minutes
▾

Most tourists attempt to walk up the steep hill from Karaköy to Istiklal Avenue. Don't. The F2 Funicular whisks you up in exactly 2 minutes. Take it every time. The hill is brutal, especially in June heat after a full day of sightseeing. Istanbulkart only.

⛴
Ferries — Eminönü Hub
Cross to Asia · Most scenic transport
▾

Istanbul's ferries run from Eminönü (near Galata Bridge) to Kadıköy (Asian side, 20 min), Üsküdar (15 min), and the Princes' Islands (1 hour). The ferry is the most romantic way to experience Istanbul — standing on deck with a tea, watching the skyline. All Istanbulkart.

Customs & Respect
Culture, Dress & Etiquette
Istanbul is warm and welcoming — a few customs will make your experience exceptional
🕌 Mosque Protocol — Mandatory
Dress Rules — both of you, every mosque
  • Shoulders: Must be fully covered. No sleeveless tops.
  • Knees: Must be fully covered. No shorts or short skirts.
  • Ivy: Headscarf required. Carry a lightweight one in your bag at all times in Sultanahmet.
  • Shoes: Remove at the rack INSIDE the mosque — NOT on the street. Walk in clean socks to the rack, then remove shoes. Stepping on prayer carpet in street-soiled socks is disrespectful.
  • Blue Mosque Friday: Closed to visitors during Friday prayers — open again after ~14:30.
What to carry in your day bag
  • Lightweight pashmina or scarf (Ivy — always)
  • A cardigan or shirt you can layer over sleeveless tops
  • Clean socks (both)
🤝 Social Etiquette — The Important Ones
⚠ Most Important: Nose Blowing
Blowing your nose loudly at a restaurant table is considered deeply offensive in Turkish culture. If you need to, excuse yourself quietly to the bathroom. This is the single most common way tourists accidentally offend.
Haggling in Bazaars
Expected and welcomed in Grand Bazaar and street markets. Start at ~50% of asking price. Warm, respectful dialogue — not confrontational. Fixed-price shops (malls, modern boutiques) do NOT haggle.
Tea / Coffee Hospitality
Shop owners and even strangers may offer you çay (tea). Accepting is a social gesture. You are not obligated to buy anything. Refusing politely is fine — a smile and "Teşekkürler" is enough.
Alcohol
Legal and widely available in licensed bars and restaurants. Do not drink in open public spaces like squares or parks. Perfectly acceptable in your hotel, rooftop restaurants, and licensed establishments.
Photography
Always ask before photographing people. Inside mosques, be discreet — never photograph during prayer times. Most sights allow photography; some interior museum pieces may be restricted.

Hidden Gems & Unexpected Moments
🌅
Sunrise from your hotel window
Your Sea View suite faces the Marmara. Set one alarm early on any morning — the light on the water at dawn in June is extraordinary. You don't need to leave the room.
🚶
Get deliberately lost in Balat
Istanbul's colourful old Jewish and Greek neighbourhood. Pastel-painted houses on steep streets, cats everywhere, small galleries and cafés. A short walk or tram from Sultanahmet.
🐱
Istanbul's famous cats
Istanbul has thousands of beloved street cats — they're a city institution, fed and cared for by locals. You'll encounter them in mosques, markets, and cafés. They are essentially co-owners of the city.
🍵
Order çay everywhere, always
"Bir çay lütfen" — one tea please. Turkish çay in a tulip glass costs almost nothing and buys you a seat, a pause, and a very Turkish moment. Do it constantly.
